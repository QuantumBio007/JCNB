JCNB Concept Note 
1.	Background and purpose
In many Latin American countries, oncologists and hospital pharmacies face a challenging combination: recurring shortages of essential cancer drugs, unpredictable deliveries, and growing reliance on informal channels when the formal system fails. In more fragile settings, this can also open the door to diverted or counterfeit products, putting already vulnerable patients at additional risk.
At the same time, ministries of health, cancer programs, and large hospitals are under pressure to manage limited budgets and complex supply chains, often with incomplete and fragmented data.
JCNB Biotech Consulting is exploring a focused initiative to address a specific part of this problem:
•	Quantifying the risk of oncology drug shortages under different geopolitical, economic, and supply-side scenarios.
•	Designing practical options and future “traceability-ready” data flows that could support better planning and safer supply, including the possibility of blockchain-based traceability where appropriate.
This document is a concept note to invite feedback from colleagues in Argentina, Venezuela, and the region.

3.	What we do
We help ministries of health, cancer programs, large public hospitals, and NGOs answer three basic questions:
1.	Where and when are we most at risk of shortages for essential oncology drugs?
2.	Given our constraints, what options do we have to reduce that risk, and what would they cost?
3.	How could future traceability systems, including blockchain, be designed so that the data actually support these decisions?
Our work centres on:
•	Scenario-based risk and forecasting models for a defined basket of essential oncology and other “critical” drugs.
•	A traceability “blueprint” that specifies which events and data should be captured in the supply chain, so existing or future systems, including blockchain, can provide meaningful, trusted visibility.
We are not trying to solve all structural issues, such as pricing, patents, or global manufacturing incentives. We focus on the part that can be treated rigorously: supply-chain risk, forecasting, and data flows.
4.	Niche focus
4.1 Geography
We start with one primary country (for example, Argentina or Colombia) and then adapt the framework to neighbours (for example, Venezuela, Panama, Peru) with country-specific parameters.
4.2 Domain
We focus on essential oncology and other critical drugs, for example:
•	Platinum agents (for example, cisplatin, carboplatin).
•	Anthracyclines (for example, doxorubicin, epirubicin).
•	Key targeted therapies and supportive medicines, where available.
These are the drugs where shortages are most damaging for survival and quality of life.
4.3 Structural issues we address
Our clients tend to face:
•	Macroeconomic volatility: currency and inflation shocks that disrupt procurement and contracts.
•	Import concentration: dependence on active pharmaceutical ingredients and finished products from a small number of countries and manufacturers.
•	Opaque, fragmented data: limited visibility across national procurement, distributors, and hospitals, making it hard to see where risk is building up.
5.	Core services (concept)
5.1 Oncology Drug Shortage Risk Assessment (Country / Region Level)
This is a structured assessment of shortage risk for a basket of approximately 10–20 essential oncology medicines in a specific country.
Scope
•	Select essential oncology drugs using WHO/PAHO lists and national formularies.
•	Model their supply and demand behaviour under current conditions and under realistic stress scenarios.
Inputs
•	Public or sharable data on:
– Registered products and local availability.
– Import dependence and supplier concentration.
– Historical or reported shortages and substitution practices.
– Treatment guidelines and estimated patient volumes.
•	Defined geopolitical and macro scenarios, for example:
– Export restrictions on a key active ingredient or finished product.
– Currency devaluation affecting import capacity.
– Regional logistics disruptions, such as port closures or strikes.
Outputs
•	Modeled shortage risk under:
– Baseline conditions.
– Three to four clearly defined shock scenarios.
•	For each drug:
– Estimated percentage of demand unmet.
– Relative vulnerability ranking.
•	Policy and procurement levers, such as:
– Recommended minimum buffer-stock levels.
– Supplier and region diversification targets.
– Candidates for regional pooled procurement or special access mechanisms.
A simple example is to simulate what happens to cisplatin and doxorubicin availability if one key exporting region imposes export restrictions and the local currency devalues, and then estimate what stock levels or diversification would be needed to avoid treatment interruptions.
5.2 Hospital Oncology Supply Resilience Simulation (Institution Level)
This provides a tailored, quantitative view of how one to three major hospitals or cancer centers would perform under supply stress.
Inputs
•	Actual or realistically approximated:
– Demand patterns by regimen and line of therapy.
– Lead times and supplier performance.
– Ordering practices and storage constraints.
– Basic budget parameters.
Outputs
•	Hospital-level indicators under baseline and shock scenarios:
– Frequency and duration of stockouts.
– Number and cost of emergency orders.
– Expiry-related waste.
– Budget impact and trade-offs.
•	Suggested inventory and ordering policies, aligned with:
– National procurement rules.
– Institutional risk tolerance and operational capacity.
•	Simple decision tools, such as Excel tables or a lightweight web interface, so local staff can explore “what-if” scenarios.
5.3 Blockchain-Ready Data and Traceability Blueprint (Second stage – optional)
We design a data and event architecture for oncology drug traceability that can be implemented by existing systems or blockchain platforms.
What we specify
•	Events to capture along the chain, such as:
– Batch and quality release.
– Import and customs clearance.
– Transfers between national stores, distributors, and hospitals.
– Storage conditions and key handoffs.
– Dispensing events at an appropriate level of aggregation.
•	Actors and responsibilities:
– Regulators and health authorities.
– Importers and distributors.
– Hospital pharmacies and cancer centers.
•	A data model for a secure shared system:
– What is stored in a shared ledger, for example, a blockchain, versus local systems.
– How privacy and access control would work.
– How it integrates with existing ERP, laboratory information, and warehouse systems.
How it links to risk and forecasting
We show concretely how better, tamper-evident data can:
•	Improve lead-time and reliability estimates in the risk models.
•	Enable earlier detection of non-delivery, diversion, or counterfeit product.
•	Support more targeted recalls and interventions when problems arise.
This is a design and advisory service; implementation is done by local integrators or global platforms.
